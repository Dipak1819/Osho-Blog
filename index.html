Osho's Perspective
of Life

Reflections on impermanence, presence, and the quiet art of being alive

I   On Impermanence

Everything passes —
even the storm inside you

There is a kind of freedom that only comes when you stop fighting what is temporary. Most of us build our lives around permanence — permanent love, permanent success, permanent safety — and in that chase we miss the singular beauty of the present moment. I remember standing in the middle of a crowded street and suddenly seeing it: every person around me was a world of their own, rushing toward something that would one day end. And yet — how alive they all looked doing it.

"The flower does not mourn its own wilting.
It simply blooms, fully, while it can."

Impermanence is not a tragedy. It is the very engine of meaning. If this conversation could last forever, would you be truly present in it? If the sunset never faded, would you bother to watch? The clock is not your enemy. It is the quiet collaborator that makes every moment irreplaceable.

II   On Presence

The only moment
that ever exists is now

We spend so much of our lives in rehearsal — rehearsing conversations that haven't happened, replaying ones that are long over. The mind is a faithful storyteller, but it is almost never telling you the story of right now. Presence is not a technique or a discipline. It is a coming home. The tea is warm. The light is doing that thing it does in the late afternoon. Someone you love is in the next room. When did you last let that be enough?

"The mind travels vast distances in a single breath.
The body never leaves the room."

I have found that presence is not about emptying the mind — that is a myth that makes meditation feel like failure. Presence is about noticing, without judgment, where the mind has wandered to, and gently returning. Over and over. The returning is the practice. Not the stillness.

III   On Being Yourself

The world already has
everyone else — be you

There is a particular kind of loneliness in pretending to be someone you are not — and the strange thing is, it rarely fools anyone. People feel authenticity the way they feel warmth from a fire. They may not be able to name what draws them closer, but they feel it. And they feel the absence of it too. The most courageous thing a person can do is not to climb a mountain or cross an ocean, but to sit quietly with who they actually are — and decide that it is enough.

"Your peculiarities are not obstacles to connection.
They are the very basis of it."

Life does not ask you to be extraordinary in the conventional sense. It asks you to be specific. Specific in your passions, your grievances, your joys. The more precisely yourself you become, the more you find that others recognise themselves in you. That is the paradox at the heart of it all: the deeper you go into your own truth, the less alone you feel.
